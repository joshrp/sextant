# Space Station & Research Points

Space research produces **Space Research Points** from a station in orbit. The station consumes cargo delivered by rockets — station parts, crew supplies, electronics, asteroid booster parts, space probe parts, and crew.

Almost everything in this feature behaves like any other recipe: continuous per-minute throughput. The one thing that's different is the **space station itself**, which is a single physical object whose level you pick.

## The space station

There is one **Space Station** node. Its consumption and research output depend on the **level** you choose with the `−` `+` stepper in the middle of the node. The node count is always 1 — you can't have half a station, and you can't have two.

Levels fall into two regimes:

- **Levels 1–2 (basic):** consumes basic station parts (Parts I), crew supplies, and crew. Produces **no** research points — these levels exist for tracking the prerequisite cost of reaching L3.
- **Levels 3+ (advanced):** consumes advanced station parts (Parts II), crew supplies, crew, and Electronics IV. Produces research points.

Within each regime, consumption is **affine**: a base amount at the regime's minimum level plus a per-input increment for every additional level. So L4 = L3 base + 1 × delta, L5 = L3 base + 2 × delta, etc. The increments differ per input — Crew Supplies grows faster than Space Crew, for example — which is why each level has its own set of numbers and not a simple linear scaling.

The calculator does **not** choose the level for you. Pick the level you're building toward; the station's per-minute inputs/outputs update accordingly. If you need more research throughput, click `+` and see what it costs.

## Launches scale like any other recipe

A launch recipe takes one rocket + a fixed amount of fuel and delivers a full cargo load (e.g. one T1 rocket carries 40 Electronics IV). But you don't need a whole launch per cycle — the calculator works in **per-minute throughput**, so a 4/min Electronics IV demand becomes `4 ÷ 40 = 0.1 launches/min`, i.e. one launch every 10 minutes averaged.

That means:

- **Rocket assembly, fuel demand, and cargo consumption all scale continuously** with the launch rate. 0.1 launches/min uses 0.1 rockets, 0.1 × the hydrogen/oxygen/water, and exactly the cargo the station needs.
- **Launch pads and rocket factories are sized by building count**, not by the launch rate. The integer building count comes from the standard infrastructure constraint that every machine uses (workers, footprint) — same as any other recipe.
- **One launch pad can serve many cargo types.** Each `Launch_X` recipe is a separate option on the same `RocketLaunchPad` machine; the pad's monthly throughput is shared across whichever recipes you place. You only need additional pads if your combined throughput exceeds one pad's capacity.

## Rockets

Rocket assembly happens on the **Rocket assembly depot**. Two recipes are available:

- **Rocket T1 assembly** — cheaper, smaller cargo cap and lower fuel cost per launch.
- **Rocket T2 assembly** — more expensive build, but higher cap and better fuel economy per cargo unit.

Each launch recipe consumes one rocket of the matching tier plus a fixed amount of hydrogen, oxygen, and water. The fuel quantities are per-launch — at fractional launch rates, you consume a proportional fraction of each.

## Crew quarters

**Crew Quarters** turn workers into Space Crew at a fixed rate. Space Crew is launched to the station the same way as any other cargo. There's no separate "crew launch" — it uses the standard launch pipeline via the Virtual Space Crew cargo product.

## Tips

- If only the **T1 launch recipe** is visible in the recipe picker, click the `2 ⌄` button next to the machine icon to expand the T1/T2 tier pair. They're grouped because they're equivalent options for the same cargo.
- Levels 1 and 2 don't produce research, so leaving the station at L1 or L2 won't fulfil any RP goal — bump it to L3 or above.
- Handles for inputs not used at the current level still show on the node, but with a quantity of 0. That's normal — it keeps edges stable when you change levels.
